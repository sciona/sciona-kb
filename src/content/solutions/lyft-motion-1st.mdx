# Lyft Motion Prediction 1st Place — Multi-Modal Trajectory Forecasting from BEV Rasterization

## Problem

Multi-modal trajectory prediction from bird's-eye-view rasterized scene representations using EfficientNet/ResNet backbones with a custom 3-mode prediction head trained via negative log-likelihood loss.

## Approach

Predict where nearby cars will drive by converting road maps and vehicle histories into overhead images, feeding them through image-recognition neural networks that output three possible future paths with confidence scores.

## CDG Walkthrough

{/* <ScionaViewerPlaceholder cdgId="solution.kaggle.lyft_motion_1st" readOnly={true} /> */}

1. **BEV Rasterization** -- Draw an overhead picture of the road, nearby cars, and the ego vehicle's past path, encoding each element as a separate color channel in an image.
1. **Temporal Stacking** -- Layer overhead images from several consecutive time steps on top of each other so the network can see how things have been moving.
1. **CNN Backbone** -- Run the layered overhead image through a powerful image-recognition network to extract meaningful spatial features.
1. **Multimodal Prediction Head** -- A final layer that produces three candidate future paths for the vehicle along with how likely each path is.
1. **Multimodal NLL Loss** -- A training objective that rewards the model for making at least one of its three predictions close to reality and for being confident about the right one.
1. **Trajectory Ensembling** -- Combine predictions from multiple trained models by averaging their best guesses to get a more reliable final prediction.

## Key Insight

BEV rasterization converts heterogeneous inputs (vector maps, agent histories, ego pose) into a uniform image tensor where standard CNNs work out of the box. The multi-modal NLL loss handles the inherent multi-modality of future trajectories — the car could turn left OR right — by only penalizing the closest mode, preventing the mode-averaging problem that plagues single-output regression.

## Failure Modes

- Sparse scenes with few agents: the rasterized image is mostly empty, wasting CNN capacity on blank pixels
- Very long prediction horizons (>10s): BEV rasterization captures a fixed spatial window; distant future states may fall outside the rasterized region
- High-speed scenarios where small angular errors compound: the 3-mode output may not cover the true trajectory if modes are too spread out or too clustered
