# OSIC Pulmonary Fibrosis Progression 1st Place — Multimodal Quantile Regression with CT Image Features

## Problem

Predict lung function decline in pulmonary fibrosis patients using EfficientNet-B5 CT scan feature extraction, tabular feature engineering of baseline FVC and temporal deltas, and MLP quantile regression predicting 20th/50th/80th percentiles for confidence-aware FVC forecasting.

## Approach

Predict how a patient's lung capacity will change over time by combining features from their CT scan images with clinical measurements, training a neural network that outputs both the predicted value and how uncertain it is.

## CDG Walkthrough

{/* <ScionaViewerPlaceholder cdgId="solution.kaggle.osic_pulmonary_1st" readOnly={true} /> */}

1. **Image Feature Extraction** -- Use a pretrained image recognition model to convert 3D CT scans into a compact numerical summary that captures lung structure information.
1. **Tabular Feature Engineering** -- Compute derived numerical features from clinical data, such as time elapsed since first measurement and starting lung capacity.
1. **Feature Concatenation** -- Merge the image-derived features with the clinical features into one combined input for the prediction model.
1. **Quantile Regression Training** -- Train a neural network that predicts not just the expected lung capacity but also optimistic and pessimistic estimates, giving a natural measure of uncertainty.
1. **Confidence Interval Postprocessing** -- Convert the three percentile predictions into a final answer with error bars, where wider spread between optimistic and pessimistic predictions means more uncertainty.

## Key Insight

Quantile regression naturally produces calibrated confidence intervals that directly optimize the competition's modified Laplace log-likelihood metric. The spread between the 20th and 80th percentile predictions becomes the confidence estimate, meaning the model learns both where and how uncertain its predictions are. This is superior to predicting mean + variance separately because the quantile loss enforces ordering and avoids the distributional assumptions of Gaussian confidence.

## Failure Modes

- Quantile crossing (q20 > q50 or q50 > q80): without explicit monotonicity constraints, predicted quantiles can violate ordering, producing invalid confidence intervals
- Very small datasets (&lt;100 patients): MLP overfits even with pretrained image features, and quantile estimates become unstable
- Homogeneous CT scans (e.g., all early-stage disease): EfficientNet features may not capture subtle progression differences, collapsing to near-identical vectors
- Non-linear temporal trajectories: the linear temporal delta assumption fails for patients with rapid acceleration or stabilization of disease
